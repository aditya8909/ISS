What Is International Space Staition?

The International Space Station (ISS) is a large spacecraft that orbits Earth, provides a home for astronauts and cosmonauts, and serves as a science laboratory. It's the largest man-made structure ever put into orbit, and is four times larger than previous space stations. The ISS is a "cooperative program" between Europe, the United States, Russia, Canada, and Japan, and is operated by five space agencies representing 15 countries. The ISS's mission is to enable long-term space exploration and provide benefits to people on Earth. It has six state-of-the-art laboratories that allow scientists to study the effects of long-duration space travel on the human body and psyche. This knowledge is invaluable for future manned missions to destinations like the Moon or Mars.

Why Is ISS Important?

The ISS is a unique platform for scientific research and technology development. It provides a long-term environment for studies that can be performed for decades, with ready access by human researchers. The ISS's capabilities include:

  • Extended experiments: The ISS can perform experiments of extended duration.
  • Experimental parameter revision: The ISS can continually revise experiment parameters based on previous results
  • Experimental design flexibility: Human operators can provide flexibility in experimental design
  • Sophisticated experimental facilities: The ISS has sophisticated experimental facilities with significant power and data resources.
  • Micro and hyper gravity studies: Studies in micro and hyper gravity can help researchers better understand its effects on humans.

These capabilities are crucial to humanity's ability to improve life on Earth while pursuing opportunities in space. For example, the ISS can help prepare astronauts for crewed interplanetary missions, and NASA sees it as a key stepping stone to letting humans explore other solar system destinations such as the moon or Mars.
